# TROUBLESHOOTING GUIDE
## Frequency Warrior Cell — Failure Mode Response Protocols

This guide provides step-by-step responses for each predictable failure mode. Use these as starting points, not scripts. Adapt to your group's culture and the specific situation.

---

## FAILURE MODE 1: SPIRITUAL BYPASSING

### Quick Identification Checklist
- [ ] Shares frequently use phrases like "everything happens for a reason"
- [ ] Emotional content is immediately translated into spiritual concepts
- [ ] Pain is consistently reframed as "lesson" or "gift"
- [ ] Language feels performative or acquired (sounds like a book, not a person)
- [ ] When asked "how do you feel?" they answer with concepts, not sensations

### Response Protocol

**STEP 1: In-the-Moment Intervention (Do this first)**

Wait for a natural pause, then:

> "I'm noticing a lot of conceptual language. Before we move on—what's the felt sense in your body right now?"

Or:

> "Can you say that same thing, but without any spiritual framework? Just the raw experience?"

**Goal:** Bring them from head to body. Don't argue with the concepts. Just redirect attention.

---

**STEP 2: One-on-One Conversation (If pattern continues for 2+ sessions)**

Pull them aside (not in group):

> "I've noticed in our sessions that when you share something difficult, you often move quickly to spiritual framing. I'm curious—what would happen if you stayed with the difficulty a bit longer? Is there something about being specific that feels unsafe?"

Listen for:
- Fear of being seen as negative
- Past punishment for "complaining"
- Genuine belief that spirituality means never suffering
- Trauma that makes embodied experience overwhelming

**Possible outcomes:**
- They recognize the pattern and want to work with it → Continue with gentle in-group prompts
- They don't see it or don't want to change → Proceed to Step 3
- They reveal they need different support → Refer to therapy; discuss fit for Cell

---

**STEP 3: Group Conversation (If pattern persists and affects container)**

> "I want to name something I'm seeing, and I want to check if others are noticing it too. [Name], I've observed that your shares often move to spiritual abstraction. I think this might be creating some distance from the direct truth-telling we're practicing here. How do others experience this?"

**Critical:** This is not an attack. It's a group maintenance conversation. The goal is not to change the person but to protect the container.

**Possible group decisions:**
- Agree to gentle prompts when it happens
- Acknowledge it's a pattern and the person wants to work on it
- Determine the person is seeking something different from what the Cell offers

---

**STEP 4: Boundary (If all else fails)**

> "We've talked about this pattern several times. The Cell is specifically for direct truth-telling, not spiritual advancement. If you're not able to engage with that frame, this might not be the right container for you right now."

**This is an invitation to leave, not an expulsion.** They can choose to step out gracefully.

## FAILURE MODE 2: VALIDATION ADDICTION

### Quick Identification Checklist
- [ ] Shares often end with rhetorical questions: "Is that weird?" "Am I crazy?"
- [ ] Body language seeks eye contact for reassurance during difficult shares
- [ ] Becomes visibly distressed if not given attention/comfort after sharing
- [ ] Shares escalate in intensity when not sufficiently validated
- [ ] Other members start feeling responsible for this person's emotional state

### Response Protocol

**STEP 1: In-the-Moment Response (Do this every time)**

Give the exact same response as for everyone else:

> "Thank you for sharing."

Then move to the next person. Do NOT:
- Offer extra comfort
- Ask follow-up questions
- Validate ("No, that makes sense!")
- Extend their time

**This is not cruel. It's consistent.** You're maintaining the container.

---

**STEP 2: One-on-One Conversation (If you notice the pattern)**

> "I want to check in about something. I've noticed you seem to need something from the group that we're not providing—reassurance, validation, comfort. I'm wondering: what are you actually looking for when you share?"

Listen for:
- Unmet needs from childhood
- Recent loss or transition
- Confusion about what the group is for
- Actual crisis that needs professional support

**Direct referral script:**

> "I care about you, and I think you might need more support than this group can offer. What you're describing sounds like it needs consistent therapeutic relationship, not a weekly peer group. Have you considered working with a therapist? I'd be happy to help you find resources."

---

**STEP 3: Group Conversation (If the pattern affects others)**

> "I want to name something that might be happening. I think some of us might be feeling responsible for [Name's] emotional state in a way that's not sustainable. Can we talk about what's actually available here, and what isn't?"

**Group agreements to reaffirm:**
- We witness; we don't heal
- We don't manage each other's emotions
- If someone needs more than the container can hold, we support them getting that help elsewhere

---

**STEP 4: Boundary**

> "[Name], we've talked about what you need and what we can offer. It seems like there's a mismatch. I think you'd be better served by a different kind of support. We care about you, and we want you to get what you need—we just can't be the ones to provide it."

---

## FAILURE MODE 3: SHADOW LEADERSHIP

### Quick Identification Checklist
- [ ] Dominates decision-making even when not facilitating
- [ ] Gives unsolicited advice despite the agreement
- [ ] Others start deferring to them ("What do you think, [Name]?")
- [ ] Uses "just trying to help" to override group process
- [ ] Volunteers for extra responsibilities, creating obligation
- [ ] Shows mild irritation when not consulted

### Response Protocol

**STEP 1: Immediate Structural Intervention**

> "I'm noticing you're offering a lot of direction. We agreed to rotate facilitation, and today that's [Name]. Can we let them hold that role?"

Or:

> "We have an agreement about no advice-giving. I'm going to pause you there—can we return to witnessing?"

**Do this immediately, every time.** Don't let it accumulate.

---

**STEP 2: Structural Enforcement**

- Enforce strict rotation: If they dominated last session, they facilitate next session
- When they facilitate, they must be silent about content and only manage time/process
- If they try to add "wisdom" while facilitating, interrupt: "Facilitator role is just timekeeping today."

---

**STEP 3: One-on-One Conversation**

> "I need to be direct with you. I'm noticing a pattern where you step into leadership even when it's not your turn to facilitate. I know you want to help, but it's creating a hierarchy that contradicts our agreements. Can you help me understand what's driving that?"

Listen for:
- Anxiety when not in control
- Past experience with "incompetent" leaders
- Genuine confusion about roles
- Personality pattern that's been rewarded elsewhere

**Clear statement:**

> "For this group to work, everyone has to be willing to step back. If you can't do that, this isn't the right container for you."

---

**STEP 4: Group Conversation**

> "We have an explicit agreement about no hierarchy. I'm seeing hierarchy creep in. I want to name it so we can address it together. [Name], others—are you noticing this? How do we want to handle it?"

**Possible structural changes:**
- Stricter rotation
- Explicit "no one speaks twice until everyone has spoken once" rule
- Check-in about power dynamics at start of each session

---

**STEP 5: Boundary**

If they cannot release control:

> "We've talked about this multiple times. The Cell requires shared power. If you're not able to participate in that structure, we need to release you from the group."

---

## FAILURE MODE 4: AIRTIME MONOPOLIZATION

### Quick Identification Checklist
- [ ] Consistently exceeds time limits
- [ ] Interrupts others with "that reminds me..."
- [ ] Says "just one more thing" repeatedly
- [ ] Others start checking out during their shares (looking away, shifting)
- [ ] Quiet members rarely get to speak

### Response Protocol

**STEP 1: Structural Prevention**

- Visible countdown timer (phone on table, screen visible)
- Bell or chime at 1 minute remaining
- Clear hand signal for "time's up"

---

**STEP 2: In-the-Moment Intervention**

> "I'm going to pause you there. We agreed on 4 minutes each. [Name], you're up next."

Or if they try to continue:

> "I'm holding the time boundary. [Name], please go ahead."

**Do not apologize. Do not negotiate. Just hold the boundary.**

---

**STEP 3: One-on-One Conversation**

> "I've noticed you often speak longer than the time we agreed on. I'm curious—what's happening for you when the timer goes off? Do you not notice it, or is something else going on?"

Listen for:
- Genuine time blindness
- Anxiety that makes them lose track
- Belief that their stuff is more important
- Past environments where they had to fight for attention

**Clear agreement:**

> "Going forward, I'm going to interrupt at time. I need you to practice ending when you hear the signal. Can you commit to that?"

---

**STEP 4: Escalating Interventions**

If pattern continues:
- Reduce their time (3 minutes instead of 4)
- Require them to go last (so others aren't cut off)
- Use a physical object—only the person holding it can speak

---

**STEP 5: Boundary**

> "We've agreed on equal airtime multiple times. You're not able to hold that boundary yourself, and I'm not willing to keep enforcing it. I think you need to step back from the group until you can participate within the agreements."

---

## FAILURE MODE 5: SOCIAL HOUR DRIFT

### Quick Identification Checklist
- [ ] Sessions start late, end late, or both
- [ ] Practice gets shortened or skipped
- [ ] Truth-telling becomes "what I did this week"
- [ ] Laughter and chat fill most of the time
- [ ] No one is uncomfortable anymore
- [ ] The edge is gone

### Response Protocol

**STEP 1: Structural Reinforcement**

At the start of each session (rotating facilitator reads):

> "Before we begin, let's remember why we're here. [Read Four Agreements aloud]. Let's check in: Are we telling the truth here? Or are we being comfortable?"

---

**STEP 2: The Evaluation Conversation**

At the 3-month mark (or when you notice drift):

> "I want to pause and evaluate. Is this still serving us? Are we doing what we came here to do? Or has this become something else?"

Questions to ask:
- When did you last feel genuinely seen here?
- When did you last hear something that challenged you?
- Are we avoiding something?

---

**STEP 3: Honest Naming**

> "I feel like we've lost the thread. I'm showing up for truth-telling, and I'm getting... pleasant conversation. That's fine, but it's not why I'm here. Can we talk about what's happening?"

**Possible outcomes:**
- Group recommits to the edge
- Group acknowledges it's become social and releases the Frequency Warrior frame
- Some members leave to form a new, edgier Cell

---

**STEP 4: Graceful Transformation**

If the group wants to become a social group:

> "Let's be honest about what this is. We started as a Frequency Warrior Cell, and we've become a dinner group. That's not failure—it's evolution. Let's formally release the agreements and just enjoy each other's company."

**This is success, not failure.** Honest acknowledgment is the point.

## GENERAL PRINCIPLES

### When to intervene vs. when to let it go

| Intervene Immediately | Let It Go | Address Later |
|----------------------|-----------|---------------|
| Violation of Four Agreements | Minor awkwardness | Pattern that might develop |
| One person dominating | Silence in check-in | Energy seems off |
| Advice-giving | Different communication styles | Someone seems withdrawn |
| Confidentiality breach | | |

### The Intervention Hierarchy

1. **In-the-moment** (gentle redirect)
2. **One-on-one** (private conversation)
3. **Group conversation** (naming the pattern)
4. **Structural change** (rules, rotation, etc.)
5. **Boundary/Release** (asking someone to leave)

**Don't skip steps unless there's an emergency.** Most issues resolve at Step 2.

### Self-Check Before Intervening

Before you address a failure mode, ask yourself:
- Is this about them, or is this about my discomfort?
- Am I trying to fix them, or protect the container?
- Have I checked in with at least one other member about what they're noticing?
- Am I willing to enforce the boundary if they don't change?

**If you can't answer these honestly, don't intervene yet.**

---

*Remember: The goal is not perfect behavior. It's honest engagement with what's happening.*
