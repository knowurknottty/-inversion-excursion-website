I. THE MASTER KEYS REVEALED

You have learned the ten mudrās of the Haṭha Yoga Pradīpika. You have mapped them to the Five Dungeons. You have practiced Mahā Mudrā, Mūla Bandha, Uḍḍīyāna Bandha, and the rest.

But among these ten, two stand apart. Two are called "master keys" — not because the others are unimportant, but because these two unlock doors that the others cannot touch.

These are:

  • Khecarī Mudrā — The Sky-Walking Seal, the King of All Mudrās
  • Vajrolī Mudrā — The Thunderbolt, the Diamond Path

The HYP is explicit about their supremacy:

"Khecarī Mudrā is the king of all mudrās. There is nothing in this world like the Mudrās to attain the human goal quickly and successfully."
"Vajrolī Mudrā is the thunderbolt. By its practice, even if one has not practiced the other niyamas, one obtains success."

These are not beginner techniques. They are master keys — to be used when the basic keys have done all they can, when you stand before doors that refuse to open, when you need to access the deepest chambers of the system.

II. KHECARĪ MUDRĀ: The King of All Mudrās

The Vertical Axis Complete

In Chapter 2, we introduced the concept of the Vertical Axis — the body-energy pathway that runs from root to crown. The elemental dharanās (Earth, Water, Fire, Air, Ether) map to this axis, each unlocking a specific chakra.

Khecarī Mudrā is the completion of the Vertical Axis. It is the technique that seals the crown, that drinks the nectar, that makes the body "divine" and "immortal."

The Practice: From Physical to Subtle

Beginner Khecarī:
  • Draw the tongue back
  • Touch the tip to the soft palate behind the upper teeth
  • Breathe normally while holding this position
  • This is the "baby" Khecarī — accessible to all
Intermediate Khecarī:
  • As the tongue becomes more flexible through practice
  • Touch the tip to the hard palate on the roof of the mouth
  • Eventually reach the soft palate further back
  • This requires months or years of gradual stretching
Advanced Khecarī:
  • The frenum (the membrane under the tongue) is gradually cut
  • The tongue is stretched using salt and myrobalan
  • Eventually, the tongue turns back and touches the nasopharyngeal vault
  • This is the "full" Khecarī of the HYP — the king of all mudrās
WYRD Etymology:
  • Khecarī: From kha (space, sky, ether) + car (to move, to walk). The sky-walker. The one who moves through space.
  • Kha: The element of ether/space — the subtlest of the five elements, the container of all others.

The Physiology of Nectar

The HYP describes a specific physiological effect of Khecarī:

"When the tongue touches the 'hole from which falls the rasa (juice)... similar to ghee and honey,' the yogin can drink soma-nectar dripping from the 'moon' (Chandra) at the crown."

This is not metaphor. The soft palate on the roof of the mouth is directly below the pituitary gland — the "master gland" of the endocrine system. Stimulation of this area through Khecarī may trigger the release of specific neurochemicals:

  • DMT (dimethyltryptamine) — the "spirit molecule"
  • Melatonin — the sleep/regeneration hormone
  • Various endorphins — natural opiates

The "soma-nectar" may be the body's own psychoactive chemistry, released through specific neurological stimulation.

III. VAJROLĪ MUDRĀ: The Conservation Key

The Practice of Not-Losing

If Khecarī is the ascending practice — drawing prāṇa up through the central channel to the crown — Vajrolī is the conserving practice. It does not send energy upward. It stops energy from draining downward.

This distinction matters. The HYP does not describe these as opposing axes. They are complementary phases of a single movement: conserve below, ascend above. The river rises only when the lower gates are closed.

To say Vajrolī "completes a horizontal axis" is a useful teaching metaphor — an invented frame that helps the intellectual mind grasp the relationship — but the texts themselves do not use this framing.

What the HYP does say is unambiguous:

"Death comes by discharging bindu, and life is prolonged by its preservation. Mind controls bindu; bindu controls life."

Bindu — the concentrated life force expressed as sexual essence — is the physical basis of vitality. Every system that drains human energy wants your bindu scattered: the attention economy through overstimulation, the financial system through scarcity stress, the information environment through perpetual novelty. Vajrolī is the refusal. The closed lower gate. The energy that stays home.

The "Horizontal Axis" framework is a map. Maps are useful. Do not confuse the map with what Vajrolī actually does in the body.

The Practice: Conservation and Transmutation

The Doctrine of Bindu:
"Death comes by discharging bindu, and life is prolonged by its preservation. Mind controls bindu; bindu controls life."
Bindu means "drop" — specifically, the drop of sexual essence (semen in men, menstrual blood/ova in women). The HYP teaches that this essence is the physical vehicle of life force (prāṇa). Its conservation and redirection is the key to longevity and power. Vajrolī Technique:

The HYP describes a specific practice for those who have "fallen into the company of women" (i.e., engaged in sexual activity):

  • Through specific muscular training, learn to draw the discharged bindu back upward
  • This is done through urethral suction and internal locks (bandhas)
  • The bindu is then redirected upward through the central channel (Suṣumnā)
  • It nourishes the higher centers rather than being lost
For Women:

The HYP is explicit that women can also practice Vajrolī:

"When a woman retains her rajas by Vajrolī, she, too, becomes a yoginī with Khecarī-like knowledge of past and future."
Rajas refers to the menstrual essence. Through similar conservation and redirection, women can achieve the same results. WYRD Etymology:
  • Vajra: From vaj (to be strong, to be hard). The thunderbolt, the diamond — that which is indestructible.
  • Vajrolī: The practice that creates the thunderbolt body — indestructible, immortal, divine.

The Traditional Context: Kāpālika Variants

The HYP describes two practices related to Vajrolī that are worth knowing about, though not for personal practice outside a traditional initiatic lineage:

Sahajolī involves mixing bindu with ash (traditionally burnt cow-dung) and applying it to the body. Amarolī involves drinking the mid-stream of urine and using it in conjunction with the Vajrolī technique.

These practices belong to the Kāpālika and Aghora traditions — schools within Tantric Śaivism that deliberately transgressed social norms as a method of dissolving ego-attachment to purity and pollution. The practices were never meant as hygiene prescriptions; they were initiation shocks, rituals designed to break a specific psychological structure in a specific student, supervised by a teacher who knew that student's precise inner obstacles.

Out of that context, they are neither effective nor safe. The HYP includes them because it is a complete survey of the tradition's technology, not because it recommends them to general readers. The same text advises keeping these practices secret from "the wicked and faithless" — which is less about moral judgment than about readiness. A student who needs ego dissolution via Kāpālika transgression practices is a student with a very specific obstacle that most readers do not have.

The Vajrolī that matters for the Inversion is the core principle: conservation and redirection of life force. The bindu retention practices achievable through Mūla Bandha, sexual sublimation, and conscious energy management produce the same fundamental result — the thunderbolt body — without requiring practices that require decades of supervised preparation to do correct.
