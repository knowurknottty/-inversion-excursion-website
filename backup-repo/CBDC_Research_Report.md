# Central Bank Digital Currencies (CBDCs): Comprehensive Research Report

## Executive Summary

Central Bank Digital Currencies (CBDCs) represent one of the most significant transformations in monetary history, with 137 countries (representing 98% of global GDP) actively exploring or implementing digital currency systems as of 2025. This report examines the key dimensions of CBDC development: programmable money capabilities, China's digital yuan as the leading model, Federal Reserve and ECB initiatives, connections to digital ID systems, and the implications for financial surveillance.

---

## 1. Programmable Money and Social Credit Implications

### 1.1 What is Programmable Money?

Programmable money refers to digital currency that can be encoded with specific conditions, restrictions, and automated execution rules through smart contracts. Unlike traditional cash or even electronic bank deposits, programmable CBDCs enable:

- **Conditional payments**: Funds that can only be spent on specific categories of goods or services
- **Expiration dates**: Money that becomes invalid after a certain time period
- **Geographic restrictions**: Currency that only works in designated areas
- **Identity-linked transactions**: Payments tied to verified digital identities
- **Automated compliance**: Built-in tax collection, sanctions enforcement, or regulatory adherence

### 1.2 Social Credit System Integration

The IMF's 2025 working paper "Can Retail Central Bank Digital Currencies Improve the Delivery of Social Safety Nets?" explicitly outlines how CBDCs can transform social assistance delivery through:

**Key Capabilities:**
- **Direct ledger access**: SSN agencies can monitor all CBDC transactions in real-time
- **Welfare assessment automation**: CBDC transaction data can determine eligibility for benefits based on income thresholds and spending patterns
- **Ex post conditionality**: Smart contracts can restrict benefit usage to eligible expenses only (similar to SNAP/EBT in the US)
- **Cross-platform aggregation**: CBDC programmability can combine data from multiple payment platforms to compute comprehensive welfare scores

**Critical Quote from IMF Report:**
> "CBDC platforms would store transactions—payments made to and from CBDC wallets—on ledgers, and SSN agencies could potentially access this information for welfare assessment and eligibility decisions... Expenditures on costly or luxury items could disqualify individuals from receiving benefits."

### 1.3 Social Credit Implications

The integration of CBDCs with social credit systems creates unprecedented surveillance capabilities:

| Feature | Traditional Banking | CBDC with Programmability |
|---------|---------------------|---------------------------|
| Transaction visibility | Bank-only | Central bank + government agencies |
| Spending restrictions | Account-level | Transaction-level |
| Welfare eligibility | Periodic assessment | Real-time, continuous monitoring |
| Benefit conditions | Manual enforcement | Automated smart contracts |
| Data retention | Limited | Permanent ledger |

## 2. China's Digital Yuan (e-CNY): The Global Model

### 2.1 Development Timeline

| Year | Milestone |
|------|-----------|
| 2014 | PBOC establishes CBDC research task force |
| 2016 | Digital Currency Institute created |
| 2019 | Official pilot program launched |
| 2020 | Initial pilots in Shenzhen, Suzhou, Chengdu, Xiong'an |
| 2021 | Expansion to Yangtze River Delta, Pearl River Delta, Beijing-Tianjin-Hebei |
| 2022 | Interoperability with Alipay and WeChat Pay; Winter Olympics showcase |
| 2023 | 19th Asian Games promotion |
| 2024 | Transaction value exceeds $985 billion; 17 provincial regions covered |
| 2025 | Six-ministry guideline to promote usage; 260+ million wallets |
| 2026 | Interest-bearing wallet policy begins (January 1, 2026) |

### 2.2 Current Scale (2024-2025)

- **Transaction volume**: 16.7 trillion yuan ($2.38 trillion) as of November 2024
- **Digital wallets**: Over 260 million personal wallets
- **Geographic coverage**: 25 cities across 17 provinces
- **Daily Olympic transactions**: $315,000+ during 2022 Winter Olympics
- **Annual growth**: Transaction volume nearly quadrupled from 1.8 trillion to 7 trillion yuan (by June 2024)

### 2.3 Technical Architecture

**Two-Tier System:**
1. **First tier**: PBOC issues and redeems e-CNY through designated commercial banks (100% reserve)
2. **Second tier**: Commercial banks distribute to public through wallets

**Key Features:**
- Offline functionality via NFC technology
- Interoperability with existing payment platforms
- Compatibility with distributed ledger technology
- Interest-bearing capabilities (starting 2026)

### 2.4 International Expansion: Project mBridge

**Participants:** China, Hong Kong, Thailand, Saudi Arabia, UAE

**Achievements:**
- 2022: $22 million in test transactions across 20 commercial banks
- 2024: UAE's first cross-border CBDC transaction (Digital Dirham 50 million / $13.6 million)
- 2024: Reached Minimum Viable Product (MVP) stage—first cross-border CBDC to do so
- IATA integration: e-CNY accepted in global airline settlement system by end-2025

### 2.5 Connection to Social Credit System

China's social credit system (as of 2025):
- **80.7 billion records** collected on National Credit Information Sharing Platform
- **180 million businesses** covered
- **¥37.3 trillion** ($5.18 trillion) in facilitated financing through credit service platform

While the PBOC's Credit Reference Center (zhengxin) operates separately under financial privacy laws, the infrastructure for comprehensive financial surveillance exists and could theoretically be integrated with CBDC transaction data.

## 3. Federal Reserve and US CBDC Development

### 3.1 Current Status: Effectively Halted

**January 23, 2025**: Executive Order 14178 prohibits US agencies from establishing or promoting a CBDC absent specific legislative authorization.

**Key Provisions:**
- Federal Reserve cannot issue retail CBDC
- Agencies barred from CBDC promotion
- Research continues but no implementation path

### 3.2 Development Timeline

| Year | Development |
|------|-------------|
| 2020-2022 | Research phase begins; Federal Reserve banks conduct feasibility studies |
| 2022 | Project Hamilton (Boston Fed + MIT) demonstrates high-speed transaction capabilities |
| 2023 | FedNow instant payment system launched (July) |
| 2024 | Continued research; House bill passed to prohibit Fed retail CBDC |
| 2025 | Executive Order 14178 formally bars CBDC without Congressional approval |

### 3.3 FedNow: The Infrastructure Precedent

Launched July 2023, FedNow provides:
- 24/7 instant payments between financial institutions
- Infrastructure that "could be adapted for potential digital currency with minimal additional development" (Fed 2024 Financial Stability Report)
- NOT a CBDC—transactions are commercial bank liabilities, not central bank money

**Participation:** 35+ initial institutions, expanded adoption through 2024-2025

### 3.4 Political Opposition

- **2024 Election**: Multiple presidential candidates made CBDC a campaign issue, vowing to block "spy" currencies
- **2025 House Bill**: Passed legislation prohibiting Fed from issuing direct retail CBDC
- **Cato Institute concerns**: Privacy, surveillance, financial autonomy

### 3.5 Wholesale CBDC Participation

Despite retail CBDC prohibition, US participates in:
- **Project Agorá**: 7-country cross-border wholesale CBDC test
- Focus: Faster global settlements, reducing SWIFT dependence

## 4. European Central Bank (ECB) Digital Euro

### 4.1 Development Timeline

| Date | Milestone |
|------|-----------|
| October 2023 | Preparation phase launched (2-year duration) |
| June 2024 | First progress report: offline functionality, holding limits, provider selection |
| November 2024 | Call for partners to test conditional payments (starting February 2025) |
| October 2025 | Governing Council decision point on progression |
| June 2025 | XRP Ledger selected for 12-month pilot program |
| Mid-2026 | Legislative framework expected |
| 2027 | Potential pilot exercise begin |
| 2028 | Target for full implementation |

### 4.2 Current Status (2025)

**Preparation Phase Activities:**
- Rulebook development
- Technology provider selection
- User research (small merchants, vulnerable groups)
- Holding limit methodology development
- Third progress report published (July 2025)

**Key Features Under Development:**
- Offline payment capability
- Privacy protections ("truly private" design goal)
- Holding limits to prevent bank deposit flight
- Conditional payments/smart contracts

### 4.3 DLT Initiatives

**Project Pontes (Short-term):**
- Links DLT platforms with TARGET Services
- Pilot scheduled Q3 2026
- 64 entities participated in 2024

**Project Appia (Long-term):**
- Integrated financial ecosystem for global settlements
- Timeline less defined
- Focus on DLT-based solutions

### 4.4 Political Context

**Support:**
- ECB President Christine Lagarde: Confirmed preparation phase on schedule (January 2025)
- Piero Cipollone (ECB Executive Board): Urgency due to US stablecoin proliferation

**Opposition:**
- French MEP Sarah Knafo: Advocates Bitcoin reserve, opposes digital euro as undermining financial autonomy
- Concerns: State control, privacy, forced adoption

### 4.5 Cost Projections

Digital euro rollout estimated to cost eurozone banks up to **€30 billion**.

## 5. Connection to Digital ID and Vaccine Passports

### 5.1 The Vaccine Passport Model

The COVID-19 vaccine passport infrastructure provides a template for CBDC identity verification:

**Vaccine Passport Features:**
- Tamper-proof QR code
- Individual name and partially masked ID
- Issuing country verification
- Phone number and unique ID verification
- Cross-border recognition

**CBDC Application:**
> "A similar system [like vaccine passport] could work for international payments with digital cash. For a cup of coffee, it should be sufficient that a national authority has verified your identity and your claim of digital currency."

### 5.2 Digital ID Integration Requirements

For CBDCs to function as designed, they require:

1. **KYC (Know Your Customer)**: Identity verification for wallet creation
2. **Biometric authentication**: Facial recognition, fingerprints for transaction authorization
3. **Unique digital identifiers**: Linking financial transactions to real-world identity
4. **Cross-border recognition**: International standards for identity verification

### 5.3 India's Model: Aadhaar + CBDC

India's integration demonstrates the convergence:
- **Aadhaar**: 99% population coverage, biometric data (fingerprints, iris scans)
- **e-Rupee**: 8 million+ users, growing 334% annually
- **Integration**: Direct benefit transfers, carbon credit generation, Unified Lending Interface

## 6. Financial Surveillance and Transaction Monitoring

### 6.1 Surveillance Capabilities

CBDCs enable unprecedented financial surveillance:

| Capability | Description |
|------------|-------------|
| **Real-time monitoring** | Every transaction visible to central bank instantly |
| **Complete history** | Permanent, immutable ledger of all financial activity |
| **Pattern analysis** | AI/ML detection of "abnormal" spending behaviors |
| **Geographic tracking** | Location data for all transactions |
| **Social network mapping** | Transaction graph reveals relationships |
| **Predictive profiling** | Risk scores based on financial behavior |

### 6.2 Official Surveillance Justifications

**Anti-money laundering (AML):**
- Real-time detection of suspicious transactions
- Automated reporting to financial intelligence units
- Cross-border transaction monitoring

**Counter-terrorism financing (CTF):**
- Frozen assets for sanctioned individuals
- Automated sanctions enforcement
- Cross-agency data sharing

**Tax compliance:**
- Automatic income reporting
- Elimination of cash-based tax evasion
- Real-time tax collection

### 6.3 The "Programmable" Risk

As documented in IMF and BIS research:

1. **Expenditure-based penalties**: "Expenditures on costly or luxury items could disqualify individuals from receiving benefits"
2. **Income-based exclusion**: Automatic loss of eligibility if CBDC-recorded income exceeds thresholds
3. **Behavioral conditioning**: Smart contracts enforce "appropriate" spending patterns
4. **Carbon tracking**: Transaction-level carbon footprint monitoring and restrictions

### 6.4 Global Surveillance Coordination

**BIS Innovation Hub Projects:**
- **Project mBridge**: Cross-border surveillance coordination (China, UAE, Thailand, Hong Kong, Saudi Arabia)
- **Project Agorá**: 7-country wholesale settlement surveillance
- **Unified Ledger vision**: BIS proposal for global financial surveillance infrastructure

## 7. Implementation Strategies and Global Timeline

### 7.1 Global CBDC Status (2025)

| Status | Count | Examples |
|--------|-------|----------|
| **Launched** | 3 | Bahamas (Sand Dollar), Jamaica (JAM-DEX), Nigeria (eNaira) |
| **Pilot** | 49 | China (e-CNY), India (e-Rupee), Russia (digital ruble), Brazil (Drex) |
| **Development** | 72 | EU (digital euro), UK (digital pound), Japan, Korea |
| **Research** | 13+ | US (halted), Canada (scaled down), Australia |

### 7.2 Projected Timeline

| Year | Expected Developments |
|------|----------------------|
| 2025 | ECB decision on digital euro progression; China e-CNY interest-bearing launch; India expansion |
| 2026 | EU legislative framework; ECB pilot possible; Russia extensive introduction |
| 2027 | ECB pilot exercise; potential UK digital pound decision |
| 2028 | Digital euro full implementation target; 15+ consumer CBDCs operational (BIS projection) |
| 2030 | 9 institutional CBDCs operational; potential US reconsideration |

### 7.3 Implementation Strategies

**Phased Approach (Most Common):**
1. **Research phase**: Technical feasibility, policy analysis
2. **Pilot phase**: Limited geographic/user testing
3. **Preparation phase**: Infrastructure build, rulebook development
4. **Launch phase**: Gradual rollout with holding limits
5. **Expansion phase**: Feature addition, interoperability

**Key Design Decisions:**
- **Holding limits**: Prevent bank deposit flight (€3,000 suggested for digital euro)
- **Intermediary model**: Bank-only vs. non-bank participation
- **Offline functionality**: Accessibility vs. surveillance trade-off
- **Interest-bearing**: Store of value vs. payment-only design

## 8. Key Risks and Concerns

### 8.1 Privacy Risks

- **Complete financial transparency** to central authorities
- **Data breaches** exposing entire transaction histories
- **Function creep** expanding surveillance beyond original scope
- **Cross-agency sharing** with law enforcement, tax authorities, social services

### 8.2 Financial System Risks

- **Bank disintermediation**: Deposit flight to CBDC during crises
- **Systemic vulnerability**: Single point of failure for entire payment system
- **Cyber attacks**: Nation-state targeting of critical infrastructure
- **Monetary policy disruption**: Direct central bank control over individual finances

### 8.3 Civil Liberties Concerns

- **Programmable restrictions** on spending choices
- **Social credit integration** linking financial behavior to privileges
- **Exclusion risks**: No CBDC = no participation in economy
- **Political targeting**: Opposition funding, protest movements

### 8.4 Geopolitical Implications

- **De-dollarization**: Project mBridge and alternatives to SWIFT
- **Currency competition**: Digital yuan vs. digital euro vs. stablecoins
- **Sanctions evasion**: CBDC networks bypassing traditional controls
- **Standard setting**: Who defines global CBDC interoperability?

---

## 9. Conclusion

CBDCs represent a fundamental shift from anonymous cash to fully traceable, programmable digital money. China's e-CNY provides the working model, with 260+ million wallets and nearly $1 trillion in transaction volume. The US has effectively halted development through Executive Order, while the EU pushes toward a 2028 launch.

The convergence of CBDCs with digital ID systems (vaccine passport model), social credit frameworks (IMF welfare assessment proposals), and AI-powered surveillance creates unprecedented capabilities for financial monitoring and control. The infrastructure for programmable money—where spending can be restricted, monitored, and conditioned—is being built now.

By 2030, the BIS projects at least 15 consumer CBDCs and 9 institutional CBDCs will be operational. The question is not whether CBDCs will arrive, but what safeguards—if any—will limit their surveillance and control capabilities.
