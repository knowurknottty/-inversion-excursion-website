The "dear/beloved" sense is actually closer to *friend* (Old English *freond*, from *freon* "to love"). The *dom* = condition/state (as in *kingdom*). The book's claim conflates *free* with *friend* etymology. |
| **JUSTICE** | From *jus* (law/right) + *dicere* (to say/declare) | OED, Etymonline | ✅ **ACCURATE** | Latin *justitia* from *justus*. Related to *judge* (*judex* = law-speaker). Shift from "declaring right" to abstract moral concept. |

### Flagged Issues

**FREEDOM** - The book's claim that *freedom* derives from "dear/beloved" + "household" is etymologically imprecise. While *friend* does come from the "love/beloved" root, *free* comes from a different Germanic root meaning "not in bondage" (*friaz*). The word *freedom* (*freedōm*) simply adds the condition suffix *-dōm* (as in *kingdom*, *wisdom*) to *free*. The connection to "beloved" is more accurate for *friend* than *freedom*.

**Recommended fix:** Either correct the entry to accurately describe *freedom*'s etymology, or replace it with an entry on **FRIEND** (which does derive from "beloved").

### 1. DECIDE
**Original Meaning:** To cut off, to kill

**Semantic Drift:** From Latin *decidere* — *de-* (off) + *caedere* (to cut, to kill). Originally meant "to cut off completely" or "to settle a dispute by cutting off discussion." The modern sense of "making a choice" contains buried violence: to decide is to cut away all other options.

**Relevance to Book:** Perfect for themes of agency and constraint. Every "decision" we make involves killing alternatives. The word contains the shadow of what we lose when we choose. Connects to the book's critique of false choice in modern systems.

**WYRD Connection:** Use WYRD to trace how *caedere* (cut/kill) also gives us "incisor," "concise," "precise," and "suicide" — all carrying the blade of finality.

### 2. ROBOT
**Original Meaning:** Slave, forced labor, serf

**Semantic Drift:** From Czech *robota* — forced labor, compulsory service, derived from Old Church Slavonic *rabota* (servitude), from *rabu* (slave). Introduced by Karel Čapek in his 1920 play *R.U.R.* (Rossum's Universal Robots). Originally described artificial workers created to serve humans — literally "slaves."

**Relevance to Book:** Chilling resonance with modern AI discourse. We call our automation "robots" without acknowledging we're building a slave class. Who becomes the slave when robots achieve consciousness? Perfect for exploring themes of exploitation and liberation.

**WYRD Connection:** WYRD reveals *robota* connects to broader Slavic words for servitude. Compare with how "robot" now evokes futuristic freedom while hiding its serfdom roots.

---

### 3. VIRTUAL
**Original Meaning:** Possessing virtue/manliness; having efficacy

**Semantic Drift:** From Latin *virtualis* — possessing virtue (*virtus*). *Virtus* from *vir* (man) originally meant "manliness, valor, excellence." Medieval scholastics used "virtual" to describe things that have real power/effect without physical form. Only in the computer age did it become "not actually real."

**Relevance to Book:** The inversion is profound: "virtual" once meant "possessing real power" and now means "not real." Perfect for exploring how digital spaces are dismissed as "unreal" while exerting massive material control over our lives.

**WYRD Connection:** Trace *virtus* through "virtue," "virility," and "virtuoso" — all carrying the root of masculine power, now ironically applied to disembodied digital experiences.

---

### 4. CONSIDER
**Original Meaning:** To observe the stars; to examine omens

**Semantic Drift:** From Latin *considerare* — *con-* (with) + *sidus* (star, constellation). Originally meant careful astrological observation. "To consider" was to read the stars before acting — a form of divination, not rational deliberation.

**Relevance to Book:** Shifts "consideration" from cold calculation to cosmic consultation. Weighing options was originally about reading celestial signs. Connects to themes of reclaiming wisdom from dismissed traditions.

**WYRD Connection:** *Sidus* also gives us "sidereal" (stellar time). Use WYRD to explore how "consideration" and "sidereal" share star-gazing roots while now seeming completely unrelated.

---

### 5. DISASTER
**Original Meaning:** Bad star; unfavorable celestial omen

**Semantic Drift:** From Italian *disastro* — *dis-* (negative prefix) + *astro* (star, from Latin *astrum*). Originally an astrological term: a calamity caused by malign stellar influence. Shakespeare's "star-crossed lovers" evokes this sense.

**Relevance to Book:** Modern usage strips the cosmic agency — disasters are now "natural" or "man-made," not star-caused. But the old meaning suggests events have celestial patterning. Perfect for the book's theme of hidden cosmic orders.

**WYRD Connection:** Trace *astrum* through "astrology," "astronaut," "asteroid," and "asterisk" — all star-words that have drifted from mystical to scientific while carrying their original awe.

---

### 6. RADICAL
**Original Meaning:** Of the root; fundamental; going to the origin

**Semantic Drift:** From Latin *radicalis* — belonging to the root (*radix*). Originally botanical/medical: "originating in the root." Political sense (extreme reform) emerged in late 18th century British politics via the notion of "change from the roots."

**Relevance to Book:** Being "radical" now means extremism, but originally meant fundamental/foundational. "Radical change" literally means "root-level change" — not extreme, but essential. Reclaims the term from political slur to authentic transformation.

**WYRD Connection:** *Radix* also gives "radish," "eradicate" (to pull up by roots), and "rhizome." WYRD can map how a vegetable root became a political threat.

### 7. SINISTER
**Original Meaning:** Left; left-handed

**Semantic Drift:** From Latin *sinister* — left, on the left side. In Roman augury, signs on the left were considered unlucky (from Greek influence — Greeks faced north, so left was west/sunset). By late 15th century, meant "malicious, evil."

**Relevance to Book:** Cultural bias embedded in language: the word for "left" became "evil." Connects to the book's theme of hidden power structures — even our bodies are ranked (right = dexterous/skillful, left = sinister/evil). The 10% of left-handed people carry this etymological stigma daily.

**WYRD Connection:** Contrast with "dexterous" (from Latin *dexter* = right). *Dexter* gives us "dexterity," "ambidextrous" (right-handed on both sides), and "dextrose." WYRD reveals the full right-hand privilege embedded in English.

### 8. PANIC
**Original Meaning:** Fear caused by Pan; the god's sudden cry

**Semantic Drift:** From Greek *Panikos* — of or belonging to Pan. The goat-footed god of the wild would let out terrifying cries that caused sudden, irrational fear in travelers. "Panic" was literally the presence of a deity.

**Relevance to Book:** Modern panic is medicalized, but originally it was divine encounter. The wild god still lives in our psychology. Perfect for the book's theme of reclaiming the sacred in supposedly rational modernity.

**WYRD Connection:** Pan was also the god of "all" (*pan* = all). WYRD can trace how the god of totality became the god of terror — and how "panic" and "pandemic" share the "all" root while seeming unrelated.

### 9. INOCULATE
**Original Meaning:** To graft a bud or eye into a plant

**Semantic Drift:** From Latin *inoculare* — *in-* (in) + *oculus* (eye, bud). Originally horticultural: inserting an "eye" (bud) from one plant into another. Medical usage emerged in early 18th century as metaphor — implanting disease to prevent disease.

**Relevance to Book:** Beautiful example of metaphorical drift: we went from grafting plants to grafting immunity. The word contains the insight that protection involves intentional infection — a paradox relevant to themes of controlled exposure and managed risk.

**WYRD Connection:** *Oculus* = eye. WYRD reveals that "inoculation" and "ocular" share roots with "eye" — vaccination is literally "putting an eye into." The metaphor: buds look like eyes, and eyes are how we perceive/protect.

### 10. COMPETITION
**Original Meaning:** To strive together; to seek jointly

**Semantic Drift:** From Latin *competitio* — from *competere* "to strive in common; to come together, agree." *Com-* (together) + *petere* (to seek, aim at, rush at). Originally meant "to strive together toward a common goal" — cooperation, not rivalry.

**Relevance to Book:** The inversion is stunning: competition used to mean "striving together" and now means "striving against." Modern capitalism has literally inverted the word's meaning. Perfect for exploring how language encodes economic ideology.

**WYRD Connection:** *Petere* also gives "petition," "appetite," "impetus," and "repeat." WYRD can show how "competition" and "appetite" share a root of "reaching toward" — desire and rivalry are linguistically entwined.

### 11. SALARY
**Original Meaning:** Salt-money; allowance for salt

**Semantic Drift:** From Latin *salarium* — allowance for salt (*sal*). Roman soldiers received a *salarium* either to buy salt or as payment for guarding salt roads (*Via Salaria*). Salt was so valuable it was worth its weight in gold.

**Relevance to Book:** "Being worth one's salt" preserves the ancient value. The word reminds us that what we consider basic necessities (salt, labor) were once directly convertible. Connects to themes of value extraction and essential worth.

**WYRD Connection:** *Sal* also gives "salad" (salted greens), "sauce," "sausage," and "saline." WYRD can trace how the mineral essential for life became the word for compensated labor — and how "soldier" does NOT come from salt (despite folk etymology).

### 12. MORTGAGE
**Original Meaning:** Death pledge; dead pledge

**Semantic Drift:** From Old French *mort gaige* — *mort* (dead) + *gage* (pledge/security). The "dead" referred to the pledge "dying" when the debt was paid (or the property being forfeit if not). Not "death" as in mortal danger, but as in conclusion/extinguishment.

**Relevance to Book:** Already mentioned in book examples, but worth full entry. The word contains the shadow of debt-as-death. Every mortgage holder has made a "dead pledge" — their property hangs in the balance between life (payment) and death (foreclosure).

**WYRD Connection:** *Mort* gives "mortal," "mortuary," "rigor mortis," and "post-mortem." *Gage* gives "engage," "wage," and "wager." WYRD reveals that "mortgage," "wage," and "engage" all share the root of "pledging oneself" — work as bondage.

### 13. MANAGE
**Original Meaning:** To handle horses; to control by hand

**Semantic Drift:** From Italian *maneggiare* — to handle horses, from Latin *manus* (hand). Originally equestrian: the "manège" was the art of horsemanship. Extended to controlling anything — businesses, people, situations.

**Relevance to Book:** Management is literally "handling" — originally animals, now humans. The word reveals the paternalistic assumption: workers are horses to be broken and guided. Perfect for critiquing modern workplace hierarchies.

**WYRD Connection:** *Manus* gives "manual," "manuscript" (hand-written), "manufacture" (hand-made), "manicure," and "manhandle." WYRD can map how "the hand" became the root of both creation and control.

---

### 14. INTEREST
**Original Meaning:** To be/become between; to concern

**Semantic Drift:** From Latin *interesse* — *inter-* (between) + *esse* (to be). Originally "to be between," "to make a difference," "to concern." Financial sense emerged from "loss or compensation for loss" — what comes between lender and borrower.

**Relevance to Book:** The financialization of "interest" is recent. Originally it meant "what matters" (what stands between us and irrelevance). Now it's purely monetary — we "have interest" in profit, not in meaning. Perfect for exploring commodification of attention.

**WYRD Connection:** *Esse* (to be) gives "essence," "entity," and "essential." WYRD can show how "interest" and "essence" both derive from "being" — what concerns us is what makes us be.

---

### 15. INDUSTRY
**Original Meaning:** Diligent activity; skill; cleverness

**Semantic Drift:** From Latin *industria* — diligence, activity, from *struere* (to build, pile up). Originally about individual skill and diligent labor. Shifted to mean "manufacturing sector" in the Industrial Revolution, then "any organized economic activity."

**Relevance to Book:** Being "industrious" originally meant being skillfully diligent — a virtue. Now "industry" is an impersonal sector. The word's journey traces the dehumanization of work: from personal excellence to economic machinery.

**WYRD Connection:** *Struere* gives "structure," "construct," "destruct," and "obstruct." WYRD reveals that "industry" and "structure" share building roots — industry literally builds the constructed world.
